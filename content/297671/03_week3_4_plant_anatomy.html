Plant Anatomy: Roots, Stems, Leaves, Flowers, and Fruit

Roots

Roots serve four primary functions: anchoring the plant, absorbing water and minerals, storing food reserves, and in some species, vegetative propagation. Most horticultural crops have one of two root system types:

  • Taproot system: A single dominant root that grows vertically downward, with smaller lateral roots. Common in dicots. Examples: carrot, parsnip, dandelion. Taproots are often used for food storage.
  • Fibrous root system: A dense network of similarly sized roots with no dominant central root. Common in monocots. Examples: grasses, onion, corn. Fibrous roots are excellent for preventing soil erosion.

The zone of active water and nutrient absorption is the root hair zone, just behind the root tip. Root hairs are tiny extensions of epidermal cells that dramatically increase surface area. The very tip of the root is protected by the root cap, which produces a slimy mucilage to ease the root's passage through soil.

Stems

Stems provide the structural framework of the plant and serve as the conduit for water, nutrients, and sugars between roots and leaves. Key stem structures include:

  • Nodes: Points on the stem where leaves, buds, or branches attach.
  • Internodes: Sections of stem between two nodes.
  • Axillary buds: Buds located in the axil (angle) between a leaf and the stem, capable of developing into branches or flowers.
  • Apical bud (terminal bud): The growing tip of the main stem; responsible for primary growth (elongation).

Stems are classified as herbaceous (soft, green, non-woody — e.g., tomato, basil) or woody (hardened with lignified tissue — e.g., apple, rose). Modified stems in horticulture include bulbs (onion), corms (gladiolus), tubers (potato), and rhizomes (iris).

Leaves

Leaves are the primary sites of photosynthesis. A typical leaf consists of the blade (the flat, broad surface), the petiole (the stalk connecting blade to stem), and in some species, stipules (small leaf-like structures at the petiole base).

Leaf arrangement on the stem is an important identification characteristic:

  • Alternate: One leaf per node, alternating sides (e.g., apple, elm).
  • Opposite: Two leaves per node, directly across from each other (e.g., maple, mint).
  • Whorled: Three or more leaves per node arranged in a ring (e.g., bedstraw, some lilies).

Leaf margins (edges) are described as entire (smooth), serrated (toothed), lobed, or deeply dissected, and are used in plant identification.

Flowers

Flowers are the reproductive structures of angiosperms. A complete flower contains all four whorls:

  1. Sepals (calyx): Outermost whorl; typically green and leaf-like; protect the flower bud.
  2. Petals (corolla): Often brightly coloured to attract pollinators.
  3. Stamens (androecium): Male reproductive organs, consisting of a filament topped by a pollen-producing anther.
  4. Pistil (gynoecium): Female reproductive organ, consisting of the stigma (pollen-receiving surface), style (stalk), and ovary (which contains the ovules).

An incomplete flower lacks one or more of the four whorls. A perfect flower contains both stamens and pistil (e.g., tomato, apple). An imperfect flower contains either stamens or pistil but not both (e.g., cucumber, squash — which have separate male and female flowers on the same plant).

Pollination and Fertilisation

Pollination is the transfer of pollen from the anther to the stigma. It may be achieved by wind (anemophily), insects (entomophily), birds (ornithophily), or artificial means. Self-pollination occurs within the same flower or between flowers on the same plant. Cross-pollination requires pollen from a different plant and is often necessary for fruit set in crops such as apples and pears.

Fruit

In botanical terms, a fruit is the mature ovary of a flower, typically containing seeds. Common fruit types in horticulture:

TypeDescriptionExamples
BerryFleshy fruit with seeds embedded in pulpTomato, grape, cucumber
DrupeFleshy fruit with a hard stone (pit) enclosing the seedPeach, cherry, plum
PomeFleshy fruit derived partly from the receptacleApple, pear
Legume (pod)Dry fruit that splits along two seamsBean, pea
AcheneSmall, dry, one-seeded fruitStrawberry surface seeds, sunflower

Note: many culinary "vegetables" are botanically fruits because they develop from the ovary of the flower — for example, tomatoes, peppers, squash, and cucumbers.
